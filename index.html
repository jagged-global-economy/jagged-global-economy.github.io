How will frontier AI exposure differ across countries? We study 141 national labor markets by combining occupation-level AI exposure scores with employment data. Our score measures how much of a country's current workforce is in jobs whose tasks frontier AI can already help perform. It captures workforce structure, not forecasts of job loss, wages, or AI adoption.

National AI Exposure

The map shows the national exposure score for each measured country. Darker shading means more of the workforce is in AI-exposed jobs. Click a country to compare its exposure and see the main worker groups behind the score.

Key Findings

  1. Exposure is regionally uneven because workforces differ. Europe & Central Asia are 50% more exposed than Sub-Saharan Africa, and white-collar employment share strongly predicts national exposure.
  2. Women are more exposed than men in 91% of studied countries. The gender gap reflects where men and women work, especially women's concentration in white-collar and sales occupations.
  3. Exposure predicts observed AI adoption. National exposure aligns with usage measures published by Anthropic, OpenAI, and Microsoft, despite differences in each source's metric.
  4. Remittances create an indirect exposure channel. For remittance-dependent economies, household income can be tied to the labor markets where migrants work, even when the workers' families live elsewhere.

Explaining Exposure

White-collar employment share is a strong structural predictor of national AI exposure. The relationship helps explain why richer regions tend to be more exposed, while keeping the mechanism grounded in labor composition rather than income alone.

Predicting AI Adoption

National AI exposure predicts adoption statistics from Anthropic, OpenAI, and Microsoft, even though the three organizations publish different types of country-level usage measures. The Anthropic panel uses a log-spaced y-axis.

Indirect Exposure Through Remittances

Remittances are earnings sent home by migrants. When those transfers are large, local income can depend on labor markets where migrants work. The remittance-accounted score reweights exposure by sender economies, so it captures an indirect exposure channel rather than job-loss risk at home. Points above the dashed line have higher remittance-accounted exposure than direct domestic exposure.

Tajikistan

Direct exposure is 0.222, while remittance-accounted exposure rises to 0.300. Remittances are 47.9% of GDP, and Russia accounts for 79.0% of covered bilateral inflows.

Central America-US corridor

Honduras, Guatemala, and El Salvador receive 82.4%, 91.5%, and 89.6% of covered bilateral inflows from the United States. Their remittance-accounted exposures rise to roughly 0.317-0.319, compared with direct exposures of 0.231-0.251.
